> Do governance structures exist?
>
> That is the question most organizations ask.
>
> It is not the question that matters most.
>
> Many organizations have boards, policies, internal controls, compliance programs, risk management systems, whistleblowing channels, and reporting mechanisms. Yet serious governance failures, fraud, and corruption still occur.
>
> So the harder question is this:
>
> Do those exercising authority possess the competence, integrity, and decision-making effectiveness required to use those structures responsibly?
>
> This question is at the center of Entrusted Authority Intelligence, an anti-corruption governance concept authored by Mike Masoud and published by The American Anti-Corruption Institute (AACI).
>
> **Entrusted Authority Intelligence** is **defined** as:
>
> The competence and judgment required to exercise entrusted authority responsibly, discharge fiduciary duties, reduce corruption exposure, manage governance risks, and support the achievement of organizational objectives.
>
> The concept rests on three interdependent **elements:**
>
> **Competenc**e — the knowledge, skills, experience, and understanding required to exercise authority responsibly.
>
> **Integrity** — the honest, ethical, and principled exercise of entrusted authority, in alignment with fiduciary responsibilities.
>
> **Decision-Making Effectiveness** — the ability to convert competence and integrity into timely, informed, accountable, and objective-oriented decisions.
>
> The **central proposition** is straightforward:
>
> **The quality of governance cannot exceed the quality of Entrusted Authority Intelligence possessed by those entrusted with authority.**
>
> This does not diminish the importance of governance structures. Boards, controls, policies, committees, and compliance systems remain essential.
>
> But these structures do not operate on their own.
>
> People operate them.
>
> People interpret reports. People ask questions. People approve transactions. People challenge management. People ignore warning signs. People exercise authority.
>
> Entrusted Authority Intelligence shifts attention from the existence of authority to the competence, integrity, and decision-making effectiveness required to exercise it responsibly.
>
> It provides a practical lens for boards of directors, executive management, public officials, regulators, trustees, and governance professionals to evaluate whether authority is being exercised competently, ethically, and effectively.
>
> The concept is not about titles. It is not about ceremonial authority. It is not about the appearance of governance.
>
> It is about the human capacity required to exercise entrusted authority responsibly.
>
> Authority alone is not enough.
>
> The real question is not whether governance structures exist.
>
> The question is whether those entrusted with authority are equipped to use it.
>
> Where have you seen this gap play out — structures in place, but authority exercised poorly?
